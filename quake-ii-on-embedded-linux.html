Quake II on Embedded Linux

Another project I worked on during my time as an undergraduate was porting and running Quake II on an embedded Linux system. I started this project with a group during Spring of 2025 for my embedded systems design class, CprE 488, at Iowa State University as our final project. The goal for this project was to experiment with PetaLinux, an embedded Linux distribution, and see how well we could get Quake II to run on our Xilinx Zynq-7000 platform, which would require a lot of work on both the hardware and software sides of the system.
While my teammates focused on the hardware acceleration and cross compilation parts of the project, my main contribution was getting a working framebuffer and graphics libraries onto our distribution so that we could get video output and be able to render the program. I started this stage by splicing together an existing VGA output framework that we had developed in a previous lab with the AXI VDMA core on the FPGA. This gave us the hardware path needed for video, but we still needed a way for Quake II to actually write to the screen. In order to make this possible I explored the different graphics libraries and framebuffer devices we could utilize on PetaLinux and eventually landed on DirectFB which would also allow us to support SDL2 for rendering. Setting this part up required a deep dive into the Yocto build configuration to include the right packages and dependencies, but after digging through documentation and experimenting for a few hours, I figured it out.
Overall, this project was my first real experience working with Linux, and it turned out to be an excellent opportunity to take what I had been learning in my classes and apply it to a much larger and complex system. On top of gaining valuable experience with the small details of embedded systems and Linux, I also learned how to troubleshoot issues and do deep research through data sheets to deliver a final product. While the final product was just a fun demonstration of an old video game running on unusual hardware, the process gave me hands-on skills that i can apply directly to real-world embedded systems projects in the future.
